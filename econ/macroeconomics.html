Gross Domestic Product

Total Income YYY: Gross Domestic Product

Consumption Expenditure CCC: the total payment for consumer goods and services.

Investment III: the purchase of new plant, equipment, and buildings and the additions to inventories are investment.

Government Expenditure GGG: governments buy goods and services from firms and their expenditure ongoods and services is called government expenditure.

Financial transfers such as unemployment benefits, Canada pension Plan benefit, and government subsidies to firms are not part of the circular flow of expenditure and income.

Exports XXX: sell domestic goods and services to the rest of the world.

Imports MMM: buy goods and services from teh rest of the world

X−MX-MX−M is called net exports.

Calculating GDP

Y=C+I+G+(X−M)Y = C+I+G+(X-M) Y=C+I+G+(X−M)

Nominal and Real GDP

Nominal GDP values output using current prices. It is not corrected for inflation.

Real GDP values output using the prices of a base year. Real GDP is corrected for inflation.

GDP Per Capita

GDP Per Capita is the nominal GDP divided by the population.

Real GDP Per Person is the real GDP divided by the population.

Real GDP, Expansion, and Recession

Busincess Cycle is a periodic but irregular up-and-down movement of total production and other measures of economic activities.

Expansion is a period during which real GDP increases (from a trough to a peak).

Recession is a period during which real GDP decreases (its growth rate is negative for at least 2 successive quarters.)

Labours and Unemployment

Labour Force and Classification of Labour

Working-Age Population: the total numbr of people aged 15 years and over.

Labour Force: the sum of employed and the unemployed.

Employed: one must have either a full-time job or a part-time job.

Unemployed: one must be available for work and must be in one of the 3 categories.

  1. On temporary layoff with an expectation of recall
  2. Without work but has looked for work in the past four weeks
  3. Has a new job to start within four weeks.

Not in Labour Force: people who are neither employed nor unemployed.

Underemployed Labour

Unofficial definition of unemployment (not used by Statistics Canada).

Discouraged Searcher: a person who currently is neither working nor looking for work but has indicated that he wants a job. In other words, he/she has looked for work sometime in the recent past but has stopped looking because of repeated failure.

Long-Term Future Starts: someone with a job that starts more than four weeks in the future is classified as not in the labour force.

Involuntary Part-Timers: part-time workers who would like full-time jobs and can't find them are part-time unemployed.

Types of Unemployment

Frictional Undereployment: the unemployment that arises from the normal labour turnover - from people entering and leaving the labour force and from the ongoing creation and destruction of jobs.

Structural Unemployment: the unemployment that arises when changes in technology or international competition change the skills needed to perform jobs or change the locations of jobs. Structural unemployment usually lats longer than frictional unemployment as workers must retrain and possibly relocate to find a job.

Cyclical Unemployment: a worker is laid off because of the economy is in a recession, and gets rehired some months later when the expansion begins has experienced cyclical unemployment.

Natural Unemployment: arises from frictions and structural change when there is no cyclical unemployment, i.e. natural unemployment is the sum of frictional and structural employment.

Natural Unemployment Rate: natural unemployment as a percentage of the labour force.

Full Employment: situation in which the unemployment rate equals the natural unemployment rate.

Causes of Natural Unemployment

Age Distribution of the Population: an economy with a young population has a large number of job seekers every year and has a high level of frictional unemployment. An economy with an aging population has fewer new job seekers and a low level of frictional unemployment.

Scale of Structural Change: the same jobs using the same machines remain in place for many years. But sometimes there is a technologial upheaval.

Real Wage Rate: real wage rates that bring unemployment are a minimum wage and efficiency wage. Efficiency Wage is a wage set above the going market wage to enable firms to attract the most productive workers, get the to work hard, and discourage them from quitting.

Unemployment Benefits: Unemployment benefits increases the natural unemployment rate by lowering the opportunity cost of job search.

Real GDP and Unemployment

Potential GDP: the real GDP at full employment (see definition).

Output Gap: the gap between real GDP and potential GDP. Normally negative.

Output Gap=Real GDP−Potential GDPOutput\ Gap = Real\ GDP - Potential\ GDP Output Gap=Real GDP−Potential GDP

Note, when the unemployment rate is less than the natural unemployment rate, real GDP is greater than potential GDP, and the output gap is positive.

Price Level, Inflation, and Deflation

Price Level: the average level of prices and the value of money.

Inflation: a persistently rising price level.

Deflation: a persistently failing price level.

Negative Impacts of Unexpected Inflation Or Deflation

Anticipated inflation or deflation isn’t a problem, but an unexpected burst of inflation or period of deflation brings big problems and costs.

Redistributes Income: unexpected inflation raises prices but doesn't raise wages. Workers are worse off and employers are better off.

Redistributes Wealth: unexpected inflation shrinks the values of the loan. Borrowers are winners and lenders are losers. The interest paid on the loan doesn't compensate the lender for the loss in the value of hte money loaned.

Lower Real GDP and Employment: unexpected inflation causes temporary boom in firms' profit, investment, production, and employment and the real GDP. Eventually, the GDP falls below potential GDP and hte unemployment rises.

Diverts Resources from Production: unpredictable inflation or deflation diverts resources from productive activities to forecasting inflation. It can become more profitable to forecast the inflation rate or deflation rate correctly than to invent new product.

Hyperinflation: an inflation rate of 50% a month or higher that grinds the economy to a halt and causes the society to collapse. Fortunately it is rare.

Consumer Price Index: mesures the price level. A measure of the average of the prices paid by urban consumers for a fixed basket of consumer goods and services.

Reference Base Period: the CPI is defined to equal 100 at this period. Currently, the reference base period is 2002.

CPI Shortcomings

New Goods Bias: you might somehow compare the price of a computer today with that of a typewriter in 1970. Because a PC is more expensive than a typewriter was, the arrival of PC might puts an upward bias into the CPI and its inflation rate.

Quality Change Bias: cars and many other goods get better every year. Part of the rise in the prices of these goods is a payment for improved quality and is not inflation. CPI, however, counts the entire price rise as inflation and overstates inflation.

Commodity Substitution Bias: changes in relative prices lead consumers to change the items they buy. If the price of a close substitute rises, people are gonna buy the cheaper alternatives. The CPI, however, still calculates the basket counting the more expensive substitute.

Outlet Substitution Bias: when confronted with higher prices, people use discount stores more frequently and convenience stores less frequent. This phenomenon is called outlet substitution. CPI does not monitor discounts and outlet substitutions.

Core Inflation

Core Inflation Rate is the inflation rate excluding volatile elements and reveal the underlying inflation trend. The most commonly used measure of Core Inflation Rate is the core CPI inflation rate. The core CPI inflation rate is calculated as percentage change in CPI excluding food and fuel. The price of these two items are among the most volatile.

Finance, Saving, and Investment

Money is what we use to pay for goods and services and factors of production and to make financial transactions.

Finance describe the activity of providing the funds that finance expenditures on capital.

Physical and Financial Capital

Physical Capital is the tools, instruments, machines, uildings and other items that have been produced in the past an that we are used to today to produce goodsand services. The term capital usually refers to physical capital.

Financial Capital is the funds that firms use to buy physical capital.

Investment increases the quantity of capital.
Deprecation decreases the quantity of capital.

Gross and Net Investment

Gross Investment is the total amount spent on new capital.

Net Investment is the change in the value of capital.

Net Investment=Gross Investment−DepreciationNet\ Investment = Gross\ Investment - Depreciation Net Investment=Gross Investment−Depreciation

Net worth, Insolvency and Illiquidity

A financial institution's net worth is the market value of what it has lent minus the market value of what it has borrowed.

A firm is Insolvent if its net worth is negative, and the firm will go out of business.

A firm is Solvent if its net worth is positive, the firm can remain in business.

A firm is Illiquid if it has made long-term loans with borrowed funds and is faced with a sudden demand to repay more of what it has borrowed than its available cash.

A financial institution can be solvent but illiquid.

Wealth and Saving

Wealth is the value of all the things that people own.

Saving is the amount of income that is not spent on consumption goods and services, nor tax paid. Saving increases wealth.

Capital Gain: market value of assets rises - increases wealth.
Capital Loss: market value of assets falls - decreases wealth.

Financial Capital Markets

Loan Markets and Mortgage

Businesses often want short-term finance to buy inventories or to extend credit to their customers. Sometimes they get this finance in form of a loan from a bank.

Household often want finance to purchase big ticket items (e.g. house). they get this finance as bank loans.

These funds are usually obtained as a loan that is secured by a Mortgage.

Mortgage is a legal contract that gives ownership of a home to the lender in the event that borrower fails to meet the agreed loan payments.

Bond Market

Bond is a proise to make specified payment on specified dates. When a person buys a newly issued bond, he may hold the bond until the borrower has repaid the amount borrowed or sell it to someone else.

The term of bond mght be long (decades) or short (months). Corporations often issue very short-term bonds as a way of getting paid for their sales before the buyer is able to pay.

When Walmart expands its business and opens new stores, it gets the finance it needs by selling bonds. Governments also raise finance by issuing bonds.

Bond Market: bonds issued by corportions and governments are traded in the bond market.

Mortgate-Backed Security: another type of bond which entitles its holder to the income from a package of mortgages. The holder of a mortgage-backed security is entitled to receive payments that derive from the payments received by the mortgage lender from the home buyer-borrower.

Stock Markets

Stock is a certificate of ownership and claim to the firm's profits.

When Petro-Canada wants finance to edxpand its business, it issues stock. Petro-Canada has issued 484 million shares of its stock. If you own 484 Petro-Canada shares, you would own one-millionth of the firm and be entitled to receive one-millionth of its profits.

Unlike a stockholder, a bondholder does not own part of the firm that issued the bond.

A Stock Market is a financial market in which shares of stocks of corporations are traded.

Financial Institutions

A Financial Institution is a firm that operates on both sides of hte markets for financial capital.

Banks accept deposits and use the funds to buy government bonds and other securities and to make loans.

Trust and Loan Companies provide similar services to banks and the largest of them are owned by banks. They accept deposits and make personal loans and mortgage laons. They also administer estates trusts, and pension plans.

Credit Unions and Caisses Populaires are banks that are owned and controlled by their depositors and borrowers. They are regulated by provincial rules, and operate only insdide their own provincial boundaries. These institutions are large in number but small in size.

Pension Funds receive the pension contribution of firms and workers, they use these funds to buy a diversified portfolio of bonds and stocks that they expect to generate an income that balances risk and return. The income is used to pay pension benefits. Pension funds can be very large and play an active role in the firms whose stock they hold.

Insurance Companies provide risk-sharing services. They enter into agreements with households and firms to provide compensation in the event of accident, theft, fire and a host of other misfortunates. They receive premiums from their customers and make payments against claims.

Financial Assets is the collective name of stocks, bonds, short-term securities, and loans.

The Loanable Funds Market

The loanable funds market is the aggregate of all the individual financial markets.

National Saving and Investment

National Savings is the total income in the economy that remains after paying for consumption and government purchase. National Savings is denoted SSS, and is the portion of national income that is not used for consumption or government purchases.

S=I=Y−C−GS = I = Y-C-G S=I=Y−C−G

Alternatively,

Where III is income (saving), YYY is GDP, TTT represents the net taxes, CCC is consumption, and GGG is government expenditure.

Net Taxes are the taxes paid to governments minus the cash transfers received from the government (such as social security and unemployment benefits).

Private and Public Saving

Private Saving is the portion of households' income that is not used for consumption or paying taxes.

Public Saving is the tax revenue less government spending.

Budget Deficits and Surpluses

Budget Surplus is an excess of tax revanue over government spending, which means positive public saving.

Budget Deficit is a shortfall of tax revenue from government spending, which means negative public saving.

Nominal and Real Interest Rate

The nominal interest rate is the number of dollars that a borrower pays and a lender receives in interest in a year.

The real interest rate is the nominal interest rate minus the inflation rate.

Loanable Funds Demand and Supply

Demand is Investment, Supply is Savings.

The Quantity of Loanable Funds Demanded is the total quantity of funds demanded to finance investment, the government budget deficit, and international investment or lending during a given period.

The Demand for Loanable Funds is the relationship between the quantity of loanable funds demanded and the real interest rate.

The higher the real interest rate, the lower the quantity of loanable funds demanded, and vice versa.

The Quantity of Loanable Funds Supplied is the total funds available from private saving, the government budget surplus, and international borrowing during a given period.

The Supply of Loanable Funds (SLF) is the relationship between the quantity of loanable funds supplied and the real interest rate.

The higher the real interest rate, the greater is the quantity of loanable funds supplied, and vice versa.

When expected profit changes, the demand for lonable funds changes. In other words, the greater the expected profit from new capital, the greater is the amount of investment and the greater the demand for lonable funds.

A household's Disposable Income is the income earned minus net taxes. When disposable income increases, consumption expenditure increases (but less than the increase in income). Some of the increase in income is saved.

The higher a household's Expected Future Income, the smaller is its saving today.

The higher a household's wealth, the smaller is its saving. If a person's wealth increases because of a capital gain, the person sees less need to save. When house pricse were rising rapidly, wealth increased despite the fact that personal saving decreased.

The risk that a loan will not be repaid is called Default Risk. The greater that risk, the higher is the interest rate needed to induce a person t lend and the smaller is the supply of loanable funds.

Effect of an Increase in Demand

If the profits that firms expect to earn increase, they increase their planned investment and increase their demand for loanable funds to finance that investment. With an increase in the demand for loanable funds, but no change in the supply of loanable funds, there is a shortage of funds.

As borrowers compete for funds, the interest rate rises and lenders increase the quantity of funds supplied.

Effect of an Increase in Supply

If one of the influences on saving plans changes and increases saving, the supply of loanable funds increases. With no change in the demand for loanable funds, the market is flush with loanable funds. Borrowers find bargains and lenders find themselves accepting a lower interest rate. At the lower interest rate, borrowers find additional investment projects profitable and increase the quantity of loanable funds that they borrow.

Government in the Loanable Funds Market

Government enters the loanable funds market when it has a budget surplus or budget deficit. A government budget surplus increases the supply of loanable funds and contributes to financing investment; a government budget deficit increases the demand for loanable funds and competes with businesses for funds.

PSLF: Private Supply of Loanable Funds

SLF: Supply of Loanable Funds (after government involvement)

PDLF: Private Demand for Loanable Funds

DLF: Demand for Loanable Funds (after government involvement)

Government Budget Surplus and Deficit

A government budget surplus increases the supply of loanable funds. The real interest rate falls, which decreases household saving and decreases the quantity of private funds supplied. The lower real interest rate increases the quantity of loanable funds demanded and increases investment.

A government budget deficit increases the demand for lonable funds. The real interest rate rises, which increases household saving and increases the quantity of private funds supplied. But the higher real interest rate decreases investment and the quantity of lonable funds demanded by firms to finance investment.

Surplus Deficit
Real Interest Rate Decrease Increase
Private Saving (Supply) Decrease Increase
Private Investment (Demand) Increase Decrease

Crowding-Out Effect

Government deficit causes massive decrease in private investment (raised real interest rate).

The crowding-out effect does not decrease investment by the full amount of the government budget deficit, because a higher real interest rate induces an increase in private saving that partly contributes toward financing the deficit.

Ricardo-Barro Effect

The Ricardo-Barro effect holds that both of the effects we’ve just shown are wrong and the government budget has no effect on either the real interest rate or investment.

Barro says that taxpayers are rational and can see that a budget deficit today means that future taxes must be higher and future disposable incomes smaller. With smaller expected future disposable incomes, saving increases today. The private supply of loanable funds increases to match the quantity of loanable funds demanded by the government. So the budget deficit has no effect on either the real interest rate or investment.

Most economists regard the Ricardo-Barro view as extreme.

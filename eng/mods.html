The Concept of Stress

Classification of Forces

External Forces

Applied load, reactions, weight ...

Traction Forces: applied oer an area (bodies in contact)

Body Forces: act on all particles over the volume

Internal Forces

Internal shear, internal moments ... Only in the form of traction (distributed load).

Stress is the intensity of the internal force at a point.

Stress-Strain Diagram and Ductility

Stress-Strain Curve

Characteristic Regions in a Stress-Strain Curve:

  • Elastic Region
  • Yield Stress Point
  • Yield Region
  • Strain Hardening
  • Ultimate Stress Point
  • Necking
  • Rupture Point (Fracture Stress σf\sigma_fσf​)

Modulus of Toughness: the entire area under the stress-strain curve.

Mudulus of Resilience: the area under the curve in the linear region.

Strain Energy Density: the area under the curve at any point represents the amount of energy stored in the material per unit volume.

Unlike engineering stress, true stress is measured per current area as oppose to the initial area used for engineering stress calculation. It therefore always continues to rise up to the point of rupture.

Ductile Material

Ductile Material has the following characteristics.

  • Will deform considerably before rupture, showing a major change in shape.
  • Necking is a main characteristic of ductile material.
  • Shear Stresses are responsible for deformation and final rupture.

Brittle Materials

Brittle Material have the following characteristics:

  • Will not deform much before rupture.
  • Rupture happens suddenly without any prior notice.
  • Behave almost elastically before rupture.
  • No permanent deformation or change in shape.
  • Much stronger in compression than tension.
  • Normal Streses are responsible for rupture.

Plastic and Elastic Behavior

Elastic Deformation: strain will disappear when the stress is removed.

Plastic Deformation: strain will not return to zero when the stress is removed.

Material Properties

Isotropic and Anistropic materials

Isotropic Materials: stress'strain curve may or may not depend on the direction. Consider samples machined from a raw material at different orientations and tested under tension/complression.

Anisotropic Materials: behavior of isotropic materials are independent of the direction. Same behavior in all directions: St, Al, Copper ...

Orthotropic Materials: grains align in one direction, e.g. Bone, Wood.

Planar Isotropic: Cold-rolled/hot-rolled sheet, strip or plate.

Statistically Indeterminate Problems

When some members of a force diagram are overly supported, there will be more unknowns than the number of equilibrium reactions.

Analysis Procedure:

  1. write down all available equilibrium equations.
  2. Insert new equations by relating strain displacements caused by the stresses.
  3. Hopefully with the inserted equations, the problem will no longer be statistiacally indeterminate.

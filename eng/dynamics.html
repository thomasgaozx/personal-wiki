Impact

Impact occurs when two bodies collide with each other during a very short period of time, causing relatively large (impulsive) forces to be exerted between the bodies.

Central Impact

Central impact occurs when the direction of motion of the mass centers of the two colliding particles is along a line of impact passing through the mass centers of the particles. The line of impact is perpendicular to the plane of contact.

During the collision the particles must be thought of as deformable. The particles will undergo a period of deformation such that they exert an equal but opposite deformation impulse ∫P⃗dt\int \vec P dt∫Pdt on each other.
Afterward, a period of restitution occurs, and the particles will either return to their original shape, or remain permanently deformed. The equal but opposite restitution impulse ∫R⃗dt\int\vec R dt∫Rdt pushes the particles apart from one another.
The deformation impulse will always be greater than the restitution impulse, i.e. ∫Pdt>∫Rdt\int P dt > \int R dt∫Pdt>∫Rdt

Let vvv denote the speed of the two particles during the collision. That is vvv is the velocity of both objects after the deformation impulse and before the restitution impulse.

Conservation of Angular Momentum

When the angular impulses acting on a particle are all zero, the angular momentum is conserved.

∑(HO)1=∑(HO)2\sum(\bold H_O)_1=\sum(\bold H_O)_2 ∑(HO​)1​=∑(HO​)2​

Obviously, if no external impulse is applied to the particle, both linear and angular momentum will be conserved. In some cases, however, the particle’s angular momentum will be conserved and linear momentum may not. An example of this occurs when the particle is subjected only to a central force, e.g. the impulsive central force FFF is always directed toward point OOO. Hence, the angular impulse (moment) created by FFF about the zzz axis is always zero, and therefore angular momentum of the particle is conserved about this axis.

Absolute Motion Analysis

A general motion can be completely specified by knowing both the angular rotation of a line fixed in the body and the motion of a point on the body.

Instantaneous Center of Zero Velocity

Instantaneous Center (IC) of Zero Velocity is a point on or off the body that has zero velocity at a specific instant in time.

Recall the relative velocity equation for general motion vB=vA+ω⃗×rB/A\bold v_B = \bold v_A + \vec\omega\times\bold r_{B/A}vB​=vA​+ω×rB/A​. Choosing the instantaneous center of zero velocity (IC), we get rid of the translation vA\bold v_AvA​ term and renders the equation

vB=ω⃗×rB/IC\bold v_B = \vec\omega\times\bold r_{B/IC} vB​=ω×rB/IC​

If IC is determined, we can easily determine one of vB,ω,rB/ICv_B, \omega, r_{B/IC}vB​,ω,rB/IC​ given the other two.

To locate IC: we can use the fact that the velocity of a point on the body is always perpendicular to the relative-position vector directed from IC to the point.

  • Known velocity of one point vA\bold v_AvA​ and angular velocity. (ez)
  • Known velocities (non-parallel) of 2 points vA,vB\bold v_A, \bold v_BvA​,vB​. (ez)
  • Known velocity (parallel) of 2 points. Use magnitude and similar triangle to solve.

Centrode: the locus of points which define the location of IC during the body's motion. Each point on the centrode acts as the IC for the body only for an instant.

Although the IC may be conveniently used to determine the velocity of any point in a body, it generally does not have zero acceleration and therefore should not be used for finding accelerations of points in a body.

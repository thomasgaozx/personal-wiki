Uncertainty - Stress and Strength

  • Strength (SSS) is the property of material or mechanical element, SyS_ySy​ is yield strength, SxyS_{xy}Sxy​ is shear yield strength, SuS_uSu​ is ultimate strength, SeS_eSe​ is endurance strength
  • Stress (σ,τ\sigma,\tauσ,τ) is a state property at a specific point within a body.

Need maximum stress to be less than strength by a sufficient margin (factor of safety). This is to account for uncertainties, the goal is for failure to be rare. Some sources of uncertainty include:

  • Variations in material composition
  • Variations in properties
  • Intensity and distribution of loading
  • Intensity of stress concentration
  • Effect of wear
  • Effect of corrosion

There are two methods to account for uncertainty:

Deterministic Method: design factor ndn_dnd​, calculated for each parameters include load, stress, deflection, etc.

  • finding appropriate design factor given known uncertainty: "loss of function" means a value that will cause failure; "maximum allowable" means value allowed for design

Standard numbering systems for various steels:

Unified Numbering Systems for metals and alloys (UNS) is produced by society of automotive engineers (SAE) and later adopted by American Iron and Steel Institute (AISI)

Letter prefix for material:

  • Carbon and alloy steel →\rightarrow→ G
    • First two numbers indicate composition
    • Third adn forth number number indicate nominal carbon content
    • Fifth number is used for special situations
  • Aluminium alloys →\rightarrow→ A
    • First number indicates processing
    • Second number indicate main alloy group
    • Third number is used to modify original alloy or designate impurity limits
    • Fourth and fifth number refer to other alloys used within base group
  • Copper-based alloys →\rightarrow→ C
  • Stainless or corrosion-resistant steels →\rightarrow→ S
    • Uses older AISI designation for first three numbers, first number indicates approximate composition
    • Fourth and fifth numbers are reserved for special purposes
    • Sometimes referred to by alloy content, e.g. 18-8 stainless steel →\rightarrow→ 18% chromium and 8% nickel

American Society for Testing and Materials (ASTM) numbering system is widely used for cast iron. It is based on tensile strength, and the number refers to minimum tensile strength (e.g. ASTM20 has minimum tensile strength of 20 kpsi).

Dynamic Failure

Fatigue failure due to dynamically cycling stresses occurs due to formation of cracks and the propagation of these cracks. Crack formation occurs in locations that experience repeated applications of locally high stress and high strain. It occurs at locations with discontinuities, geometric changes (stress concentrations).

Crack propagation is broken down to three stages:

  1. Nucleation to Micro-crack: initiation of microcracks; due to cyclic plastic deformation; typically not visible to human eye
  2. Micro-crack to Macro-crack: cracked services open and close, rub together; beach marks can be observed: plateau-like fracture surfaces separated by longitudinal ridges; parallel to crack front, perpendicular to direction of crack growth
  3. Sudden, Fast Fracture: remaining cross section cannot support loading; sudden fast fracture occurs; It can be brittle, ductile or a combination

Stage I: crack nucleation

  • Presense of localized plastic strain: breaking of some atomic bonds; atoms in crystal planes slip past one another
  • Leads to formation of slip bands: typically occur on plane of maximum shear stress, i.e. 45∘^\circ∘ to loading direction
  • Persistent slip bands can lead to extrusions and intrusions: acts as stress concentrations and can become microcrack

Stage I to Stage II transition point:

  • Crack forms a stress concentration at its tip: tensile plastic zone
  • Crack can be opened by a tensile normal stress

Stage II: crack grows in direction perpendicular to direction of applied load.

Dynamic Failure Methods

  • Low cycle fatigue has relatively high cyclic loads with mostly plastic stresses and strains; short lives
  • High cycle fatigue has relatively low cyclic loads with mostly elastic stresses and strains; long lives (>104>10^4>104)

There are 3 major dynamic failure methods:

  • Stress-life method predicts number of cycles to failure (N)(N)(N) under a specific loading condition: the focus for this course, considers stage I to III of crack nucleation and propagation, based on empirical data as opposed to theory; least accurate; traditional, easy to implement for wide range of design application; adequately assesses high-cycle fatigue applications.
  • Strain-life method also predicts #cycle to failure: focus on Stage I, crack nucleation. It is based on empirical data and good for low-cycle fatigue applications with high strains.
  • Linear-elastic fracture mechanics (LEFM) method: focus on Stage II - crack propagation; it assumes crack is already present adn detected; teh goal is to predict crack growth

Fatigue design criteria:

  • Infinite-life design: design for stresses that never exceed the endurance limit; plastic strain is almost entirely avoided; applications that need to sustain over a million stress cycles; used with stress-life method
  • Safe-life design: designed for finite life (<1<1<1 million cycles); applications may have limited stress cycles or imprecision in estimating fatigue life; uses significant safety margins; used with stress-life and strain-life methods
  • Fail-safe design: approach to design where if one part fails, the entire system does not fail; not trying to entirely prevent crack initiation and growth
  • Damage-tolerant design: assumes a crack is present; used with LEFM method; aimed at dictating inspection and replacement schedule

Kinematics in Mechanism

Degree of Freedom (DoF), also known as mobility, is the number of independent parameters that are needed to uniquely define a system's position in space at any instant in time.

Types of motion:

  • Pure translation:
    • rectilinear
    • curvilinear
  • Pure rotation
  • General plane motion

Linkage: basic building blocks of all mechanisms, made of links and joints.

  • Link: rigid body with at least two nodes.
  • Nodes: points for attachment to other links
  • Joint: connection between two or more links at their nodes, which allows some motion, or potential motion between the connected links

Joint classifications:

  • Type of contact between elements: line, point, surface
    • Lower pair: surface contact
    • Higher pair: point or line contact
  • Number of degrees of freedom allowed
    • 1 DoF: full joint
    • 2 DoF: half joint
    • 3+ DoF
  • Type of physical closure
    • Form closed: closed by its geometry
    • Force closed: external force keeps joint together
  • Number of links joined.

Terminologies:

  • Kinematic Chain: an assemblage of links and joints interconnected in a way to provide a controlled output motion in response to a supplied input motion.
  • Mechanism: a kinematic chain in which at least one link has been grounded to the frame of reference
  • Machine: a collection of mechanisms arranged to transmit forces and do work

Type of Links:

  • Ground: a link that is fixed with respect to the reference frame
  • Crank: a link that makes complete revolutions and is pivoted to ground.
  • Rocker: a link that has oscillatory rotation and is pivoted to ground.
  • Coupler: a connecting rod, a link with complex motion and is not pivoted to the ground.

Grashof Condition and Barker Classification

Inversion of a kinematic chain grounds one of its link. A given kinematic chain will have as many inversions as it has links.

Grashof condition predicts the rotation behaviour of a four-bar linkage's inversions based on link length only, satisfying this condition means it is a Grashof linkage:

S+L≤P+QS+L\le P+Q S+L≤P+Q

  • SSS is length of shortest link
  • LLL is length of longest link
  • P,QP,QP,Q are lengths of remaining links

We consider 3 different cases:

  • Case 1: S+L<P+QS+L<P+QS+L<P+Q, Grashof linkage
    • Ground the shortest link →\rightarrow→ a double-crank
    • Ground link adjacent to shortest →\rightarrow→ crank-rocker
    • Ground link opposite to the shortest →\rightarrow→ double-rocker
  • Case 2: S+L>P+QS+L>P+QS+L>P+Q, non-Grashof linkage
    • Triple rockers for all inversions. Why triple? I see only 2 rockers and 1 coupler.
  • Case 3: S+L=P+QS+L=P+QS+L=P+Q
    • Parallelogram or antiparallelogram form gives a double-crank
    • Crank-rocker, deltoid or kite form.

Barker's Classification: don't worry about it

Balancing

Conditions for Static Balancing:

  • Sum of all forces on the moving system, including inertial forces, must be zero
  • Locate the object center of gravity at the axis of rotation

Dynamic Balancing:

  • Rotation does not produce a resultant centrifugal force or couple
  • Reduces stress on bearings

Eliminate vibrations:

  • Lower risk of mechanism failure, increase life span
  • Reduce noise and discomfort

Static Balancing

Goal: determine the mass and position vector of the balance mass that could be added to the system to statically balance the system.

Procedure:

  • Assume system is rotating at a constant angular velocity (α=0)(\alpha=0)(α=0)
  • Freeze the sytem at a specific angular position, the exact position chosen does not matter.
  • Set up a coordinate system with its origin at the center of the rotation
  • Sum all the inertial forces acting on the system (external forces are not included in the balancing)

Balance Linkage

A few notes regarding balancing the individual members:

  • Rocker and crank can both be statically balanced: we can add counterweight to the link such that the CoM is at the point of rotation (incident to ground)
  • Coupler link experiences general motion, there is no fixed pivot, and CoG is always in motion. This link cannot be balanced using the techniques we studied.

Berkof-Lowen Method: makes the global CoM (CoG for all links combined) stationary for all linkage positions. This method will determine the counterweight mass and counterweight position for link 2 and link 4 to make the global CoM stationary. It focuses on static balance.

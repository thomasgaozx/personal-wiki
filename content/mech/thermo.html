First Law of Thermal Dynamics

An adiabatic process occurs without transferring heat or mass between a thermodynamic system and its surroundings. Unlike an isothermal process, an adiabatic process transfers energy to the surroundings only as work.

Types of energies we worry about:

  • Potential Energy (PE)
  • Kinetic Energy (KE)
  • Internal Energy (U): all microscopic form of energy

Types of functions:

  • Path functions have inexact differentials designated by the symbol δ\deltaδ. Therefore the amount of heat or work is represented by δQ,δW\delta Q, \delta WδQ,δW, respectively.
  • Point functions depend on the state only (path independent) have exact differentials designated by symbol ddd. A change in specific volume is represented by dvdvdv.

∫12dv=Δv,∫12δW=W12\int_1^2 dv= \Delta v,\qquad \int_1^2\delta W=W_{12} ∫12​dv=Δv,∫12​δW=W12​

Properties of pure substances

Terminologies:

  • Compressed Liquid/Subcooled Liquid: water in liquid state
  • Saturated Liquid: a liquid that is about to vaporize
  • Saturated Vapor: a vapor that is about to condense
  • Saturated Liquid-Vapor Mixture: liquid and vapor coexist in equilibrium
  • Superheated Vapor: a vapor that is not about to condense
  • Saturated Temperature: at a given pressure, the temperature at which a pure substance changes phase
  • Saturation Pressure: at a given temperature, the pressure at which a pure substance changes phase
  • Triple Point: a state at which all three phases of water coexist
  • Triple Line: the triple line have the same pressure and temperature but different specific volumes.

Compressibility Factor - Measuring Deviation from Ideal Gas Behavior

Gases deviate from ideal-gas behavior significantly at states near the saturation region and the critical point. This deviation from ideal-gas behavior at a given temperature and pressure can accurately be accounted for using compressibility factor ZZZ, defined as

Z=PvRTPv=ZRTZ=vactualvidealZ=\frac{Pv}{RT}\qquad Pv=ZRT\qquad Z=\frac{v_\textrm{actual}}{v_\textrm{ideal}} Z=RTPv​Pv=ZRTZ=videal​vactual​​

Gases behave differently at a given temperature and pressure, but they behave very much the same at temperatures and pressures normalized with respect to their critical temperatures and pressures. The normalization is done as

  • Reduced Pressure PRP_RPR​
  • Reduced Temperature TRT_RTR​

    PR=PPcrTR=TTcrP_R=\frac{P}{P_\textrm{cr}}\qquad T_R=\frac{T}{T_\textrm{cr}} PR​=Pcr​P​TR​=Tcr​T​

  • Principal of corresponding States: ZZZ factor is approximately the same at the same reduced pressure and temperature.
  • Generalized compressive chart: can be used for all gases (see below), and have the following properties
    • PR≪1P_R\ll 1PR​≪1, gasses behave as ideal gases regardless of temmperature
    • TR>2T_R>2TR​>2, ideal gas behavior can be assumed with good accuracy regardless of temperature
    • deviation of a gas from ideal-gas behavior is greatest in the vicinity of critical point.

  • Pseudo-reduced specific volume vRv_RvR​: used when P-v, T-v are given instead of P-T. Check A-15 appendix to determine PR,TRP_R,T_RPR​,TR​.

    vR=vactualRTcr/Pcrv_R=\frac{v_\textrm{actual}}{RT_\textrm{cr}/P_\textrm{cr}} vR​=RTcr​/Pcr​vactual​​

Case Study: Mixing Gases

Consider two stationary, rigid, and adiabatic gas tanks A and B, separated by a partition (state A). We now remove the partition and arrive at state B where the two gases are mixed.

  • Arrive at the same temperature and pressure, use ideal gas law to list two equations and solve.
  • Is work done by A or to A? If initial temperature of A is greater than B, then heat transfer necessarily take place from A to B, and work transfer occurs from B to A. Thus work is done to A.
  • Is assumption of ideal gas correct? Check compressibility factor Z.

Second Law of Thermodynamics

Thermal energy reservoir: a hypothetical body with a relatively large thermal energy capacity (mass x specific heat) that can supply or absorb finite amounts of heat without undergoing any change in temperature.

Heat Engine

  • Source: a reservoir that supplies heat
  • Sink a reservoir that absorbs heat

Heat Engines: converts heat to work.

  1. Receive heat from high-temperature source
  2. Convert part of the heat to work
  3. Reject the remaining waste heat to a low-temperature sink
  4. Operate on a cycle

Working fluid: the fluid used by cyclic devices like heat engine. The P-V diagram of the fluid undergoes a cycle.

Even the most efficient heat engines reject almost one-half of the energy they receive as waste heat.

Refrigerator and Heat Pump

Refrigerators: transfers heat from a low-temperature medium to a high-temperature one. Uses refrigerant as working fluid.

Heat Pumps: transfers heat from low-temperature medium to high-temperature one, but the objective is to maintain high-temperature.

In any cases, we have conservation of energy:

Wnet,in=QH−QLW_\textrm{net,in}=Q_H-Q_L Wnet,in​=QH​−QL​

Coefficient of Performance COP\mathrm{COP}COP is the ratio of desired output to required input:

{COPR=QL/Wnet,in=(QH/QL−1)−1refrigeratorCOPHP=QH/Wnet,in=(1−QL/QH)−1heat pumpCOPHP=COPR+1\begin{cases} \mathrm{COP_{R}}=Q_L/W_\textrm{net,in}=(Q_H/Q_L-1)^{-1}&\textrm{refrigerator}\\ \mathrm{COP_{HP}}=Q_H/W_\textrm{net,in}=(1-Q_L/Q_H)^{-1}&\textrm{heat pump}\\ \mathrm{COP_{HP}=COP_R+1} \end{cases} ⎩⎪⎪⎨⎪⎪⎧​COPR​=QL​/Wnet,in​=(QH​/QL​−1)−1COPHP​=QH​/Wnet,in​=(1−QL​/QH​)−1COPHP​=COPR​+1​refrigeratorheat pump​

Statements of the Second Law

Second Law of Thermodynamics: the two statements are equivalent

  • Kelvin-Plank Statement: It is impossible for any device that operates on a cycle to receive heat from a single reservoir and produce a net amount of work.
  • Clausius Statement: It is impossible to construct a device that operates in a cycle and produces no effect other than the transfer of heat from a lower-temperature body to a higher-temperature body.

Reversible and Irreversible Processes

  • Reversible Process: a process than can be reversed without leaving any trace on the surrounding. In other words, both the system and the surroundings (note the plural) are returned to their initial states at the end of the reverse process. This is possible only if the net heat and net work exchange between the system and the surrounding is zero combining the original and reverse process.
  • Irreversible: processes that are not reversible
    • Internally reversible process: no irreversibilities occur within the boundaries of the system during the process.
    • Externally reversible process: if no irreversibilities occur outside the system boundaries during the process. Heat transfer between a reservoir and a system is an externally reversible process if the outer surface of the system is at the temperature of the reservoir.
    • Totally reversible process: no irreversibilities within the system or its surroundings.
  • Irreversibilities: factors that cause the process to be irreversible:
    • Friction
    • Unrestrained expansion of a gas: a gas is separated from vacuum by a membrane, as membrane rupture, the gas fills the entire tank. From the conservation of energy considerations, it can easily be shown that the amount of heat transferred from the gas equals the amount of work done on the gas by the surroundings. The restoration of the surroundings involves conversion of this heat completely to work, which would violate the second law.
    • Heat transfer through a finite temperature difference

Carnot cycle: the best known reversible cycle, also called Carnot heat engine.

  • Process 1-2: reversible isothermal expansion
  • Process 2-3: reversible adiabatic expansion
  • Process 3-4: reversible isothermal compression
  • Process 4-1: reversible adiabatic compression

Reversed-carnot cycle: also called Carnot refrigeration cycle.

Carnot's Principles:

  1. The thermal efficiency ηth\eta_\textrm{th}ηth​ of an irreversible heat engine is always less than the efficinecy of a reversible one operating between the same two reservoirs.
  2. The efficiencies of all reversible heat engines operating between the same two reservoirs are the same.

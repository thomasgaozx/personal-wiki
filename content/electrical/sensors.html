Thermocouple Sensors

RTD and thermistors are passive devices. Thermocouples are active devices whose emf varies with temperature due to thermoelectric effects:

  • The emf is approximately linear in TTT.

Three-Wire Thermocouple

  • TMT_MTM​: measurement junction temperature.
  • TRT_RTR​: reference junction temperature.
  • An open-circuit voltage is measured that is then a function of only the temperature difference (TM−TR)(T_M-T_R)(TM​−TR​) and is approximately linear.
  • Extension wires are usually employed to move the reference to a more secure location.

  • Sensitivity: 6μV/∘C6\mu V/^\circ C6μV/∘C to 50μV/∘C50\mu V/^\circ C50μV/∘C
  • Range: (−150,1765) ∘C(-150, 1765)\ ^\circ C(−150,1765) ∘C

  • Signal conditioning: output voltage of thermalcouple is usually very small (~50 mV). In addition, the small signal levels make the devices susceptible to electrical noise. In most cases, the thermocouple is used with a high-gain differential amplifier.
  • Reference compensation: A problem with thermocouples is the necessity of knowing the reference temperature. Variation of the reference temperature leads to errors. To compensate those errors
    • Controlled temperature reference block: extension wire and put the reference in a temperature-controoled box.
    • Reference compensation circuits: Use specialized IC block to provide correction. Don't worry about it.
    • Software reference correction.

Thermocouple Noise Handling

The voltages generated generally are less than 50 mV and often are only 2 or 3 mV, and in industrial environment it is common to have hundreds of millivolts of electrical noise generated by large machines. A thermalcouple constitutes an excellent antenna for pickup of noise from electromagnetic radiation in the radio, TV and microwave bands. In short, a bare thermocouple may have many times more noise than temperature signal at a given time.

To use thermocouple effectively in industry, many techniques are employed:

  1. The extension wires from the thermocouple to the reference junction or measurement system are twisted and then wrapped with a grounded foil sheath. Twisting is down to decouple the wires from induced voltages from varying electric and magnetic fields that permeate our environment. In principle, the induced voltages are equal in each loop of the twisted wire but of opposite phase, so they cancel.
  2. The measurement junction itself is grounded at the point of measurement. The grounding is typically to the inside of the stainless steel sheath that covers the actual thermocouple. The advantege of this is that the noise voltage will be distributed equally on each wire of the TC. Then the differential amplifier will at least partially cancel this noise.
  3. An instrumentation amplifier that has excellent common-mode rejection is employed for measurement.

Pressure Sensors (p<1 atmp<1\ \textrm{atm}p<1 atm)

Under low pressure, the pressure could be determined based on the rate at which heat is radiated away from heated filament. The heat loss is proportional to the number of gas molecules per unit volume. We have thus transduced a pressure measurement to a temperature measurement.

  • Pirani Gauge: this gauge determines the filament temperature through a measure of filament resistance. Filament excitation and resistance measurement are both performed with a bridge circuit. The response of resistance versus pressure is highly nonlinear.
  • Thermocouple: this gauge measures filament temperature using a thermocouple directly attached to the heated filament. In this case, ambient room temperature serves as a reference for the thermocouple, and the voltage output, which is proportional to pressure, is highly nonlinear.
  • Ionization Gauge: range 1E-3, 1E-13 atm. This gauge employs electrons, usually from a heated filament, to ionize the gas whose pressure is to be measured, and then measures the current flowing between two electrodes in the ionized environment. The number of ions per unit volume depends on the gas pressure, and hence the current also depends on gas pressure. This current is then monitored as an approximately linear indication of pressure.

Restriction Flow Sensors

One of the most common methods of measuring the flow of liquids in pipes is to introduce a restriction in the pipe, and measuring the pressure drop that results across the restriction. When such a restriction is placed in the pipe, the velocity of the fluid through the restriction increases, and the pressure in the restriction decreases. There exists a relationship between pressure drop and rate of flow (note the nonlinearity):

Q=KΔpQ=K\sqrt{\Delta p} Q=KΔp​

where

  • QQQ: volume flow rate
  • KKK: a constanht for the pipe and liquid type
  • Δp\Delta pΔp: drop in pressure across the restriction

There are 3 common types of restrictions:

We simply need to measure the differential pressure using a DP cell to determine the flow.

Pitot Tube

The fluid will be brought to rest in the tube, and therefore its pressure will be the sum of the static fluid pressure plus the effective pressure of the flow. The pressure in the pitot tube is measured in differential to the static pressure of the flowing fluid in the same vicinity as the tube. This differential pressure will be proportional to the square root of the flow rate. The flow rate in a pipe varies across the pipe, so the pitot tube determines the flow rate only at the point of insertion.

Obstruction Flow Sensors

In a rotameter, the obstruction is a float that rises in a vertical tapered column. The lifting force, and thus the distance to which the float rises in the column, is proportional to the flow rate. The lifting force is produced by the differential pressure that exists across the float, because it is a restriction in the flow. This type of sensor is used for both liquids and gases.

A moving vane flow meter has a vane target immersed in the flow region, which is rotated out of the flow as the flow velocity increases. The angle of the vane is a measure of the flow rate. If the rotating vane shaft is attached to an angle-measuring sensor, the flow rate can be measured for use in a process-control application.

A turbine type of flow meter is composed of a f**reely spinning turbine blade **assembly in the flow path. The rate of rotation of the turbine is proportional to the flow rate. If the turbine is attached to a tachometer, a convenient electrical signal can be produced.

In all of these methods of flow measurement, it is necessary to present a substantial obstruction into the flow path to measure the flow For this reason, these devices are used only when an obstruction does not cause any unwanted reaction on the flow system.

Magnetic Flow Meter

If charged particles move across a magnetic field, a potential is established across the flow, perpendicular to the magnetic field. Thus, if the flowing liquid is also a conductor (even if not necessarily a good conductor) of electricity, the flow can be measured by allowing the liquid to flow through a magnetic field and measuring the transverse potential produced. The pipe section in which this measurement is made must be insulated, and a nonconductor itself, or the potential produced, will be cancelled by currents in the pipe. This type of sensor produces an electrical signal directly and is convenient for process-control applications involving conducting fluid flow.

A magnetic flow meter will only work with conducting fluids such as blood.

Photoconductive Detectors

In a semiconductor photodetector, a photon is absorbed and thereby excites an electron from the valence to the conduction band. As many electrons are excited into the conduction band, the semiconductor resistance decreases, making the resistance an inverse function of radiation intensity. For the photon to provide such an excitation, it must carry at least as much energy as the gap.

Photovoltaic Detectors

Photovoltaic detectors generates a voltage that is proportional to incident EM radiation intensity. They actual convert the EM energy into electrical energy.

We see that the cell is actually a giant diode that is constructed using a pn junction between appropriately doped semiconductors. Photons striking the cell pass through the thin p-doped upper layer and are absorbed by electrons in the n layer, which causes formation of conduction electrons and holes. The depletion-zone potential of the pn junction then separates these conduction electrons and holes, which causes a difference of potential to develop across the junction. The upper terminal is positive, and the lower negative. It is also possible to build a cell with a thin, n-doped layer on top so that all polarities are opposite.

Photomultiplier Tube

Photomultiplier tube is one of the most sensitive photodetectors available.

We have many electrodes called dynodes (like the cathode for photo-emissive detector) maintained at successively increasing voltage. The final electrode is the anode, which is grounded through a resistor RRR. When photo electrons strike the first dynode, it travels through subsequent dynodes and gets accelerated. This process is repeated for each dynode until the electrons that reach the anode are greatly multiplied in number. Therefore, there is a gain associated with photomultiplier tube's detection. One single photon striking the cathode may result in millions of electrons at the anode!

Signal conditioning involves using voltage divider to supply voltages at each dynode. The anode is grounded through a resistor.

Pyrometry

Blackbody Radiation

All objects having a finite absolute temperature emit EM radiation. The nature and extent of such radiation depend on the temperature of the object.

  • thermal equilibrium: an object emits as much EM radiation as it gains for its surroundings.

Blackbody radiation: consider an idealized object that absorbs all radiation impinging on it regardless of wavelength, and therefore becomes an ideal absorber. This object also emits radiation without regard to any special peculiarities of wavelength and therefore becomes ideal emitter.

Such ideal object is placed in thermal equilibrium so that its temperature is controlled, and we obtain the plot

We see that, at low temperatures, the radiation emitted is predominantly in the long-wavelength (far-infrared to microwave) region. As the temperature is increased, the maximum emitted radiation is in the shorter wavelengths, and finally, at very high temperatures, the maximum emitted radiation is near the visible band. Because of this shift in emission peak with temperature, an object begins to glow as its temperature increases.

Broadband Pyrometers

Total radiation pyrometer: a broadband pyrometer that collects radiation extending from the visible through the infrared wavelengths. (non-contact temperature measurement).

The radiation from an object is collected by the spherical mirror S and focused on a broadband detector, D. The signal from this detector, then, is a representation of the incoming radiation intensity, and thus the object’s temperature. In these devices, the detector is often a series of microthermocouples attached to a blackened platinum disc. The radiation is absorbed by the disc, which heats up, and results in an emf developed by the thermocouples. The advantage of such a detector is that it responds to visible and IR radiation with little regard to wavelength.

IR pyrometer: mostly sensative to IR. This device often uses a lens formed from silicon or germanium to focus the IR radiation on a suitable detector. IR pyrometers are often handheld, pistol-shaped devices that read the temperature of an object toward which they are pointed.

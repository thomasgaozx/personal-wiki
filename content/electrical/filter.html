Filter Transmision

The filter tranfer function H(s)\mathbf{H}(s)H(s) is the ratio of the output voltage Vo(s)V_o(s)Vo​(s) to the input voltage Vi(s)V_i(s)Vi​(s)

H(s)=Vo(s)Vi(s)\mathbf{H}(s)=\frac{V_o(s)}{V_i(s)} H(s)=Vi​(s)Vo​(s)​

The magnitude of the transmission is often expressed in decibels:

  • The gain function Av(ω)=20log⁡∣T(jω)∣,dBA_v(\omega)=20\log|T(j\omega)|,\textrm{dB}Av​(ω)=20log∣T(jω)∣,dB.
  • The attenuation function Aat=−20log⁡∣T(jω)∣,dBA_\textrm{at}=-20\log|T(j\omega)|,\textrm{dB}Aat​=−20log∣T(jω)∣,dB.

The filter-design process begins with the filter user specifying the transmission characteristics required of the filter. Observe that since a physical circuit cannot provide constant transmission at all passband frequencies, the specifications allow for deviation of the passband transmission from the ideal 0 dB, but place an upper bound, AmaxA_\textrm{max}Amax​ dB (attenuation), on this deviation. Depending on the application, AmaxA_\textrm{max}Amax​ typically ranges from 0.05 dB to 3 dB.

Also, since a physical circuit cannot provide zero transmission at all stopband frequencies, the specifications usually allow for some transmission over the stopband. However, the specifications require the stopband signals to be attenuated by at least AminA_\textrm{min}Amin​ dB relative to the passband signals. Depending on the filter application, AminA_\textrm{min}Amin​ can range from 20 dB to 100 dB.

First-Order Filter Functions

The general first-order transfer function is given by

H(s)=a1s+a0s+ω0\mathbf{H}(s)=\frac{a_1s+a_0}{s+\omega_0} H(s)=s+ω0​a1​s+a0​​

This bilinear transfer function characterizes a first-order filter with a natural mode at s=−ω0s=-\omega_0s=−ω0​, a transmission zero at s=−a0/a1s=-a_0/a_1s=−a0​/a1​. The numerator coefficients a0,a1a_0, a_1a0​,a1​ determines the type of filter (low-pass, high-pass, etc).

An important special case for the first-order filter function is the all-pass filter. Although the transimission of all-pass filter is ideally constant at all frequencies, its phase shows frequency selectivity. All-pass filters are used as phase shifters.

H(s)=a2s2+a1s+a0s2+(ω0/Q)s+ω02\mathbf{H}(s)=\frac{a_2s^2+a_1s+a_0}{s^2+(\omega_0/Q)s+\omega_0^2} H(s)=s2+(ω0​/Q)s+ω02​a2​s2+a1​s+a0​​

For a bandpass filter, the center frequency is equal to the pole frequency ω0\omega_0ω0​, The selectivity of the second-order bandpass filter is usually measured by its 3-dB bandwidth, which is the difference between the two frequencies ω1,ω2\omega_1, \omega_2ω1​,ω2​ at which the magnitude response is 3 dB below its maximum value at ω0\omega_0ω0​. Corner Frequency Loss

The corner frequency for a 0 dB/dec -> -20 dB/dec corner is 3 dB. To compensate for corner frequency loss, there are two ways:

  1. The casual way is to pad the bandwidth requirement by a factor of 10 (at the required corner), this would reduce the corner frequency loss from -3.01 dB to -0.04 dB.
  2. The standard way is to use a sallen-key filter (with complex pole and therefore peak at corner frequency). Ensure the peak is 3 dB by adjusting the quality factor QQQ.

General Filter Design Procedure*

  1. Compute stage gain ceiling using Avceiling=ft10⋅f-3dB\displaystyle A_{v_\textrm{ceiling}}=\frac{f_t}{10\cdot f_\textrm{-3dB}}Avceiling​​=10⋅f-3dB​ft​​, where f-3dBf_\textrm{-3dB}f-3dB​ is the highest corner frequency.
  2. Obtain passband gain AvpbA_{v_\textrm{pb}}Avpb​​ and stopband gain AvsbA_{v_\textrm{sb}}Avsb​​ (if any)
  3. Obtain the highpass, lowpass and possibly stopband frequencies
  4. Calculate the slope (if not given) at the corner frequency using 20Δlog⁡(Av)/Δlog⁡(f)20 \Delta \log(A_v)/\Delta \log(f)20Δlog(Av​)/Δlog(f)
  5. From the slope deduce the number of poles/zeros required
  6. Decide what filters to use. Generally,
    • A Sallen Key is required to remove corner frequency loss (Qo=1Q_o=1Qo​=1 if there is no loss)
    • A highpass/bandpass is required to remove DC offset
  7. Compute stage gain. Sallen key filters have fixed stage gain, the rest of the stage gain should be equally divided between single op amp filters. For this reason, don't use passive filters.
  8. Use inverting/noninverting amplifier to deal with additional gains.

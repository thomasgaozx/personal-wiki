Solve Circuits with Laplace

  1. Use Kirchoff's laws to set up system of differential equations.
  2. Transform each differential equation into algebraic equation by taking Laplace transform of both sides of the equation.
  3. Solve the algebraic equations to obtain the Laplace transform of the output of the circuit.
  4. Take the inverse Laplace transform to obtain the circuit output itself.

Stability

A circuit is said to be stable when the response to a bounded input signal is a bounded output signal. A circuit that is not stable is said to be unstable. Unstable circuits are potentially dangerous.

Consider a circuit represented by the transfer function H(s)H(s)H(s):

H(s)=N(s)(s−p1)(s−p2)⋯(s−pN)H(s)=\frac{N(s)}{(s-p_1)(s-p_2)\cdots(s-p_N)} H(s)=(s−p1​)(s−p2​)⋯(s−pN​)N(s)​

The pip_ipi​ are the poles of the transfer function. A circuit is stable if and only if all of its poles have negative real parts, Re{pi}<0\mathrm{Re}\{p_i\} < 0Re{pi​}<0.

The network function H(ω)\mathbf{H}(\omega)H(ω) of a stable circuit can be obtained from its transfer function H(s)H(s)H(s) by letting s=jωs=j\omegas=jω.

Single-Time-Constant (STC) Networks

An STC network is one that can be reduced to one reactive component (inductance or capacitance) and one resistance.

Observe that the transfer function of both can be expressed as a voltage-divider ratio. The impedance of the capacitor varies with frequency ZC=1/jωC\mathbf{Z}_C=1/j\omega CZC​=1/jωC. It's easy to see that for the low-pass network, lim⁡ω→∞H(ω)=0\displaystyle\lim_{\omega\rightarrow\infty}\mathbf{H}(\omega)=0ω→∞lim​H(ω)=0, thus the low pass.

To find the time constant τ\tauτ for STC circuit is really simple:

  1. Set independent voltage or current source to zero.
  2. "Grab hold" the two terminals of the reactive element (CCC or LLL) and determine the equivalent resistance RRR that appears between these two terminals.
  3. The time-constant is then CRCRCR or L/RL/RL/R.

Resonant Circuits

Recall impedance formula Z=R+jX\mathbf{Z}=R+jXZ=R+jX, where RRR is the resistance and XXX is the reactance.

If the reactance of a circuit is inductive (positive reactance) at some frequencies and capacitive (negative reactance) at other frequencies. The special frequency in between, i.e. when reactance is 0, is called resonant frequency ω0\omega_0ω0​. In other words, at resonant frequency, the impedance is purely resistive.

A parallel resonant circuit consists of a resistor, capacitor, and inductor connected in parallel.

A series resonant circuit consists of a resistor, capacitor and inductor connected in series with the power source.

First-Order Filters

Active filters contain amplifier devices to increase signal stregth.

Passive filters are made up of passive components such as resistors and capacitors and have no amplifying elements (transistors, op-amps) so have no signal gain. The output is level is always less than the input.

Second-Order Filters

Second order filters provide an inexpensive approximationto ideal filters, and are used as building blocks for more expensive filters.

The frequency response of second-order filter is characterized by 3 filter parameters:

  • the gain kkk
  • the corner frequency ω0\omega_0ω0​
  • the quality factor QQQ that controls the shape of frequency response
    • A system with low quality factor (Q<1/2Q<1/2Q<1/2) is said to be overdamped (doesn't oscillate at all).
    • A system with high quality factor (Q>1/2Q>1/2Q>1/2) is said to be underdamped (oscilation with decay).
    • A system with an intermediate quality factor (Q=1/2Q=1/2Q=1/2, complex root) is said to be critically damped (does not oscilate)

A Butterworth approximation to the ideal low-pass filter is obtained by choosing Q=0.707Q=0.707Q=0.707.
